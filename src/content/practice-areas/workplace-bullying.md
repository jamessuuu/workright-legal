faqs:
  - question: "What legally constitutes workplace bullying?"
    answer: "Under the Fair Work Act, workplace bullying is repeated unreasonable behaviour directed toward a worker that creates a risk to health and safety. It can include verbal abuse, intimidation, exclusion, unreasonable work demands, humiliation, and undermining. While one-off incidents generally don't constitute bullying, a single serious event may still give rise to other legal claims."
  - question: "Can I make a bullying claim if I'm still employed?"
    answer: "Yes. Stop bullying orders through the Fair Work Commission are specifically designed for workers who are still in their current role. These orders require your employer to take steps to prevent the bullying from continuing. You don't need to resign to take action."
  - question: "What compensation can I receive for workplace bullying?"
    answer: "Compensation depends on the legal pathway pursued. Workers compensation claims for psychological injury typically range from $50,000 to $150,000+. General protections or discrimination claims can result in additional compensation for lost wages and non-economic loss. Negotiated exit packages are another option."
  - question: "What evidence do I need to prove workplace bullying?"
    answer: "Keep detailed records of incidents including dates, times, witnesses, and what was said or done. Save emails, text messages, and written communications. Obtain medical evidence from your GP or psychologist documenting the impact on your health. Copies of workplace policies and any internal complaints are also valuable."
  - question: "What's the difference between bullying and reasonable management action?"
    answer: "Reasonable management action carried out in a reasonable manner - such as performance management, setting work tasks, or directing how work is done - is not workplace bullying, even if it causes stress. However, if management action is carried out unreasonably (for example, public humiliation during a performance review), it may cross the line into bullying."
  - question: "Should I make an internal complaint before seeking legal help?"
    answer: "Not necessarily. While internal complaints can sometimes resolve issues quickly, they can also alert the bully and lead to retaliation if not handled properly. We can advise whether an internal complaint is appropriate for your situation before you take that step."

## Compassionate Workplace Bullying Lawyers in Sydney

Workplace bullying can devastate your mental health, your career, and your life. If you're experiencing repeated unreasonable behaviour at work, you have legal options - and you don't have to face them alone.

At WorkRight Legal, we take a compassionate, trauma-informed approach to workplace bullying cases. We understand the courage it takes to speak up, and we provide safe, confidential support throughout the entire process.

## Types of Bullying We Address

Our experienced team handles all forms of workplace bullying including verbal abuse and intimidation, excessive or unjustified criticism, unreasonable work demands and impossible deadlines, deliberate exclusion and isolation, public humiliation, work undermining and sabotage, oppressive micromanagement, discrimination-based bullying, retaliation for complaints, gang bullying, and cyberbullying through digital channels.

## Available Legal Pathways

Depending on your circumstances, we may pursue stop bullying orders from the Fair Work Commission, general protections claims under the Fair Work Act, discrimination complaints through the Australian Human Rights Commission or Anti-Discrimination NSW, psychological injury workers compensation claims, or negotiated exit packages that compensate you fairly while allowing you to move on.
