faqs:
  - question: "What is the difference between direct and indirect discrimination?"
    answer: "Direct discrimination occurs when someone is treated less favourably because of a protected attribute - for example, not being promoted because of your age. Indirect discrimination occurs when a seemingly neutral workplace policy or practice disadvantages people with a particular protected attribute - such as requiring all staff to work weekends, which may disadvantage parents with family responsibilities."
  - question: "What compensation can I receive for workplace discrimination?"
    answer: "Compensation varies based on the severity and impact of the discrimination. Awards typically include economic loss (lost wages, benefits), non-economic loss (pain and suffering, humiliation), and special damages (medical expenses). Awards commonly range from $20,000 to $100,000+, with serious cases exceeding $200,000."
  - question: "What time limits apply to discrimination claims?"
    answer: "Federal discrimination complaints to the Australian Human Rights Commission must be lodged within 6 months. Fair Work general protections claims have a 21-day deadline. State anti-discrimination claims vary. These deadlines are strictly enforced, so prompt action is essential."
  - question: "What evidence do I need to prove discrimination?"
    answer: "Useful evidence includes emails, text messages, written communications showing discriminatory behaviour, witness statements from colleagues, performance reviews (especially if they changed after disclosing a protected attribute), medical evidence of psychological harm, workplace policies, and any records of complaints made internally."
  - question: "Can my employer fire me for making a discrimination complaint?"
    answer: "No. It is illegal for an employer to terminate, demote, or otherwise disadvantage you for making a discrimination complaint. This is known as victimisation, and it gives rise to a separate legal claim. If this happens to you, contact us immediately."
  - question: "Should I make an internal complaint first or go straight to an external body?"
    answer: "This depends on your circumstances. Sometimes an internal complaint can resolve the issue quickly, especially if the employer has good anti-discrimination policies. However, if you fear retaliation, or if the discrimination is systemic or involves senior management, an external complaint may be more appropriate. We can advise on the best strategy for your situation."

## Sydney's Trusted Workplace Discrimination Lawyers

Discrimination at work can be deeply damaging - affecting your career, your confidence, and your wellbeing. Australian law protects employees from discrimination based on a range of protected attributes, and employers who breach these protections can be held accountable.

At WorkRight Legal, we provide compassionate, confidential legal support to employees who have experienced workplace discrimination. We understand how difficult it can be to speak up, and we're here to guide you through every step of the process.

Our team has extensive experience with all forms of workplace discrimination including age discrimination, gender and sex discrimination, racial discrimination, disability discrimination, sexual orientation and gender identity discrimination, religious discrimination, pregnancy and family responsibilities discrimination, marital status discrimination, harassment and bullying linked to protected attributes, victimisation for making complaints, and systemic workplace discrimination.
