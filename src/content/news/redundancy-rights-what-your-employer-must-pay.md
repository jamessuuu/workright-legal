## Your legal entitlements

When your position is made genuinely redundant, Australian law sets minimum standards that your employer must meet. These are not discretionary. They are enforceable legal rights under the National Employment Standards (NES).

## Notice period

Your employer must give you written notice or pay you in lieu of notice. The minimum period depends on your length of service:

- 1 to 3 years of service: 2 weeks
- 3 to 5 years of service: 3 weeks
- 5+ years of service: 4 weeks
- If you are over 45 and have more than 2 years of service: add 1 extra week

## Redundancy pay

Redundancy pay (also called severance pay) is separate from notice. The NES minimum is:

- 1 to 2 years: 4 weeks
- 2 to 3 years: 6 weeks
- 3 to 4 years: 7 weeks
- 4 to 5 years: 8 weeks
- 5 to 6 years: 10 weeks
- 6 to 7 years: 11 weeks
- 7 to 8 years: 13 weeks
- 8 to 9 years: 14 weeks
- 9 to 10 years: 16 weeks
- 10+ years: 12 weeks

Small businesses with fewer than 15 employees are exempt from paying redundancy pay under the NES, though your enterprise agreement or contract may provide for it.

## When a redundancy is not genuine

Your redundancy may not be genuine if:

- Your employer could have reasonably redeployed you within the business
- Your role still exists but has been given to someone else
- You were selected for redundancy because you exercised a workplace right
- The consultation requirements in your award or agreement were not followed

If any of these apply, you may have grounds for an unfair dismissal claim.
