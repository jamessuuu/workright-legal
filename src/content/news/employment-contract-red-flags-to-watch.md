## Why contract review matters

Most employees sign their employment contracts without reading the fine print. This is understandable when you are excited about a new role, but the clauses in your contract can have serious long-term consequences for your career and your finances.

Here are five red flags we see regularly in employment contracts across Sydney.

## 1. Overly broad restraint of trade clauses

A restraint of trade clause limits where you can work after leaving the company. While some restraints are reasonable, we frequently see clauses that attempt to prevent employees from working in their entire industry for 12 to 24 months across the whole of Australia.

Many of these clauses are unenforceable, but they can still create uncertainty and delay when you are trying to move to a new role. A contract review can identify whether the restraint is reasonable and negotiate it down to something fair.

## 2. Vague termination provisions

Your contract should clearly state how much notice is required from both sides and the grounds on which you can be terminated. Watch out for contracts that give the employer broad discretion to terminate "at any time" or "for any reason" without adequate notice.

## 3. Set-off clauses

A set-off clause allows your employer to deduct amounts from your pay or entitlements if they believe you owe them money. While there are legitimate uses, overly broad set-off clauses can be used to withhold redundancy pay, accrued leave, or other entitlements upon termination.

## 4. Intellectual property assignments

If your contract includes a clause assigning intellectual property rights to the employer, check the scope carefully. Some contracts claim ownership of any work you create, including personal projects developed outside working hours on your own equipment. This is often broader than legally necessary.

## 5. Annualised salary arrangements

If your salary is described as "annualised" and expressed as a single figure that includes overtime, penalty rates, and allowances, you need to understand exactly what it covers. Some annualised salary arrangements result in employees being paid less than they would receive under the applicable award.
