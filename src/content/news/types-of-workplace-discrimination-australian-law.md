## What counts as discrimination at work?

Workplace discrimination in Australia is governed by the Fair Work Act 2009, the Age Discrimination Act 2004, the Disability Discrimination Act 1992, the Racial Discrimination Act 1975, and the Sex Discrimination Act 1984. These laws protect employees against discrimination based on race, colour, sex, sexual orientation, age, disability, marital status, family responsibilities, pregnancy, religion, political opinion, national extraction, and social origin.

Here are the five main types.

## 1. Direct discrimination

Direct discrimination occurs when you are treated less favourably than someone else because of a protected attribute. Examples include being passed over for promotion because of your age, being paid less because of your gender, or not being hired because of your ethnic background.

The key question is whether a reasonable person would conclude that the protected attribute was a factor in the treatment.

## 2. Indirect discrimination

Indirect discrimination happens when a workplace policy or requirement appears neutral but has a disproportionate impact on people with a particular attribute. For example, requiring all employees to work fixed hours without flexibility may indirectly discriminate against employees with caring responsibilities.

Indirect discrimination can be lawful if the employer can demonstrate that the requirement is reasonable and necessary for the business.

## 3. Harassment

Workplace harassment includes unwelcome conduct that is offensive, humiliating, or intimidating, and is related to a protected attribute. Sexual harassment is the most commonly reported form, but harassment based on race, disability, or age is also unlawful.

A single serious incident can constitute harassment. It does not need to be repeated.

## 4. Victimisation

Victimisation occurs when an employer takes adverse action against you because you made, or plan to make, a discrimination complaint. This includes dismissal, demotion, reduction of hours, or being excluded from opportunities.

Protection against victimisation is critical. Without it, employees would be afraid to report discrimination.

## 5. Adverse action

Under the general protections provisions of the Fair Work Act, it is unlawful for an employer to take adverse action against you because of a workplace right or protected attribute. Adverse action includes dismissal, demotion, transfer, or any alteration of your position to your detriment.

General protections claims carry uncapped compensation, unlike unfair dismissal claims which are capped at 6 months' pay.

## What to do if you are experiencing discrimination

Document everything. Save emails, text messages, and meeting notes. Write down dates, times, and witnesses for verbal incidents. Then seek legal advice promptly.
