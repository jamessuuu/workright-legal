## What is the right to disconnect?

The right to disconnect is a workplace right introduced under amendments to the Fair Work Act. It gives employees the right to refuse to monitor, read, or respond to contact from their employer (or third parties related to work) outside of their working hours, unless that refusal is unreasonable.

This law applies to employees of non-small business employers from 26 August 2024, and to employees of small business employers from 26 August 2025.

## What does "unreasonable" mean?

The Fair Work Commission considers several factors when determining whether a refusal to respond is unreasonable:

- The reason for the contact
- How the contact is made and the level of disruption
- Whether the employee is compensated for being available
- The nature of the employee's role and level of responsibility
- The employee's personal circumstances, including family or caring responsibilities

Emergency situations and genuinely urgent matters are more likely to make a refusal unreasonable.

## What this does not mean

The right to disconnect does not mean your employer cannot contact you outside hours. It means you are protected from adverse consequences if you choose not to respond. Your employer can still send you an email at 9pm. You are just not obligated to read or reply to it until your next working day.

It also does not affect existing on-call arrangements or contractual availability clauses, provided those arrangements are reasonable.

## What your employer cannot do

Your employer cannot:

- Discipline you for not responding to a message sent outside your working hours
- Include you in negative performance reviews because of your after-hours availability
- Pass you over for promotion or opportunities because you exercise this right
- Terminate your employment because you refused out-of-hours contact

Any of these actions could constitute adverse action under the general protections provisions of the Fair Work Act, carrying uncapped compensation.

## How to exercise this right

If you are receiving pressure to respond to after-hours communications:

1. Check your employment contract and any applicable enterprise agreement for relevant clauses
2. Raise the issue with your employer in writing
3. If the issue is not resolved, you can apply to the Fair Work Commission for a stop order
4. Keep records of all after-hours contact and any consequences for not responding
