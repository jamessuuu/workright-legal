## Building a strong case starts with documentation

One of the most common questions we hear from clients experiencing workplace bullying is: "Will anyone believe me?" The answer often comes down to evidence. A well-documented case is significantly stronger than one relying solely on verbal accounts.

## Types of evidence that support bullying claims

### Written communications

Emails, text messages, Slack messages, or any written communication that demonstrates bullying behaviour is some of the strongest evidence available. Save copies of any messages that contain threats, belittling language, unreasonable demands, or exclusion from work activities.

### Personal diary or log

Keep a detailed record of each incident as soon as possible after it occurs. Include the date, time, location, what was said or done, who was present, and how it affected you. Courts and tribunals view contemporaneous notes favourably because they are recorded close to the time of the event.

### Witness statements

If colleagues witnessed the bullying, their statements can be valuable. However, we understand that colleagues may be reluctant to come forward. An employment lawyer can advise on how to approach this sensitively.

### Medical records

If the bullying has affected your health, medical records from your GP, psychologist, or psychiatrist provide objective evidence of the impact. These records help establish the connection between workplace behaviour and your wellbeing.

### Policies and procedures

Your employer's bullying and harassment policy, code of conduct, and any internal complaint records are relevant. If your employer failed to follow their own procedures after you reported bullying, this strengthens your case.

## What to do next

If you are currently experiencing workplace bullying, start documenting incidents now. Even if you are unsure whether you want to take legal action, having a record of events preserves your options.
